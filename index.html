STORIES FOR A FRIEND



CHAPTER 1

I FIGURED THE BEST WAY to tell you the story of how I got found is to tell it like we're back on that porch in Nashville. Come back late from closing at Taqueria, all the wiring in your brain frayed and sparking because Jesus and Laticia were yelling for guac and cheese dip like GI’s for a medic and because you had to mop up and roll all that silverware before you could go. Toss your tips into the money pile in your closet. Come out for a beer and a smoke. The sun’s making golden scales out of our neighbor’s roof tiles and diamonds out of the crushed-up quartz in the asphalt. Air’s woolly with water. Strips the brown bottles of their wrappers and drips over our knuckles. There’s the great Southern critter orgy all around and all night long, the sirens, fiddles, clicks, and belches like a marching band in the middle of The Big One. Mazzy Star from your phone. Alright, you say. So talk.

Couple reasons I dropped out of school and hitchhiked to Panama, one of them being that I spent my whole life being suggested into shit that was supposed to be good for me. I pretty much did the same stuff my brother did in high school—soccer, newspaper, distance track—and I went to the same college, too. He's four years older than me so he was a fresh grad working as an analyst in San Fran when I came in as a freshman. Day of my orientation, he wrote me a list of classes I should take, said I might as well major in business like he did. I don't mean any disrespect to Cal or my brother because he was just looking out for me and because there's a lot to learn in a school like that. I know you got a lot out of your philosophy classes when you were there, like with Searle and Dreyfus, rest in peace. My experience there was with kids whose biggest interest in art was how to dress the part and professors who were talking about literary patricide and Great American Novel. But I know I only got myself to blame: I wasn’t ready to hunker down and learn, and if I could go back I'd try to get the most out of it. Maybe I'd go for philosophy like you or math like Axel did, if I’m up for it. Second reason I left was because I had just broken up with a girl named Alex. She was the realest thing that happened to me and without her just about everything around me went back to make-believe. Last reason, this sounds dumb, but the way things were going I knew I could have gone my whole life without knowing who I was, if I was strong or weak, brave or cowardly. You know how in some of the books we read when we were little there's the boy in the tribe or the village. He's not the strongest or the biggest kid, but he's got a brain and a heart, too. When he gets a little older he leaves his home for a journey. His dad's solemn about it, but he's proud because it's something he's got to do. His mom's sad, and maybe his little sister’s so mad at him that she won’t even look at him when he leaves. But he goes and runs into people who need his help, and it's scrappy, but he helps them. Months and years pass, and he becomes strong, a leader. Then he comes home and no one recognizes him anymore because he's a man now, with the glint and the shoulders and everything.

Yeah well, none of that happened to me. Most of those six months I spent in my head, walking all day and standing there with my thumb out, getting mad and sad when people would just stare at me or give me the thumbs-up back. I camped wherever I could, worked at a ranch, a hostel, on a boat, on an island, but I wasn't a great worker, and I probably took more than I deserved. The only reason I kept going was that if I couldn’t bring anything real back, I could at least tell everyone how many countries I went through. Towards the end I was getting pretty loopy—I was in my own movie, interviewing myself, talking about the scenery, laughing at my own jokes, making up conversations and fantasies about seeing my friends again, or re-doing things that I messed up with Alex. Maybe you don’t know this, but you were about the only friend I had during that time, the only person I really talked to. One time you wrote me while you were teaching English somewhere in Korea. Man, I remember where I was when I got your message, and I remember word-for-word what you said. It was the day before I got caught trying to hop a border. I was camping in a rusty truck bed behind a gas station, and I was deep in my sleeping bag with the hood cinched tight because of all the damn mosquitoes. You said the writing was good and to keep going with it. Probably didn’t mean much to you to say that, and I don’t know how I got it, seeing as how there was no connection. But I got it and I read it and I was glad to have you as my friend.

I’ll tell you more of what I got up to when I was abroad, but I want to begin on the night before I got found.





CHAPTER 2

IT WAS FALL, couple months after I got back from Panama. I’d been on my way to Albatross, but when I got to the door I realized I’d forgotten my ID. So I was walking back to Aashik and Axel’s apartment where I was crashing now. A hard rain had come all the night before and through the day, and it’d only now just stopped. It was like the great turtle that had us on its back had gone for a dip and finally come up for air, and now the whole water-logged city was dripping and draining off. Every crack and pothole brimmed, the lawns had become rice paddies, and small but mighty rivers took the curbs into the gutters that were dammed with detritus. The leaves were brown and fallen and the ones that weren't stuck to the sidewalk like wet brown paper skittered on it like a kid was pulling them along on a string. Fine specks of rain still played in the air like miniature fireflies, and they landed and lit on your face. Sometimes when you walked under a tree or an eave, a nasty fat drop would find its way on the back of your neck and down between your shoulder blades. It was five or six miles to the apartment and I hadn't brought my earbuds, so I tried to make myself sturdy for it. Put my hood on and my hands in my pockets and tried to think of something nice.

Only the homeless were out now. Man, you know how it is here. They wear clear ponchos flecked with rain and have blue tarps tied with bungee cords over shopping carts that look like they’re carrying a garage sale. After the bars have closed and before the coldest cold, you see them setting up for the night, too. Some in the recessed entrances of shopfronts, on the sidewalk, on the grass in People's Park, over the steam vents on campus. They sleep in worn sleeping bags on flattened cardboard. Most of them pull their bags over their faces, and all you see is a covered mound.

One of them didn't cover her face, and I recognized her as the woman I'd seen on my way to the bar. She’d been spreading her sleeping bag out over some cardboard. Her beanie had a pink embroidered rose, and it lay beside her. Her bare head was spun from root to tip with fine silver strands. This was somewhere on San Pablo, and I was pretty sure I hadn’t passed it, but I asked her to make sure. 

"Albatross?" I said, pointing down the street.

She nodded and pointed the same way. "But you gotta buy me dinner first." Then she cackled, showing her black chasm of a mouth, and I laughed with her.

But she was asleep now and had the sunken and puckered mouth of someone without teeth and the frown of sleeping, worn-out children who look like they’re really deep in a good dream. Her beanie was tight over her greasy bangs that curled slightly under it. Tarry flecks of mascara were caught in her lashes, and her face was tanned unevenly like someone who's been skiing with goggles. Her arms were crossed over her chest inside her green sleeping bag, which was pulled up to her chin. It was lined with red flannel and was coming apart at its footbox, the white stuffing poking out of it like it was an old stuffed animal. She was Asian. Japanese was my guess. They've been here longer so they got more time than the other Asians to fall off. She looked about 50. 

I’d seen her around since I was a freshman. She usually sat on the sidewalk of Telegraph where people set up their tents and tables to sell jewelry and Nepalese souvenirs: incense sticks, special stones, singing bowls, pipes. She always wore that same black beanie, and her eyes were always big like she was dazed. Couldn’t tell if it was because she was nice or on something. I once saw something strange happen with her, a story that stays with me because I still can’t make sense of it.

One Saturday morning a few years back Aashik and I went to get some wet burritos from Cafe Durant. The sun was warming the cool air that came in from the Bay and promised one of those idyllic days that’s all we Californians have ever known and so take for granted. The morning carried with it a pleasant lightness because it was game day. Wistful alumni and the parents of students strolled eagerly around Southside and Sproul Hall wearing their blue and gold. Black ticket scalpers on the street corners asked you if you were going to the game, and nappy-headed students in their pajama bottoms and flip flops squinted out into the sunlight from their shabby, carpeted apartments and jaywalked around the traffic to recover from their hangovers by getting a full American breakfast: eggs, bacon, thawed sausage links, thick acidic pancakes soaked in butter and syrup. From the stadium the brass and drumline of the marching band could be heard preparing for the war game.

Aashik and I took this all in from the balcony of Cafe Durant. We were under one of a dozen umbrellas, seated at tables and chairs made of wrought iron that screeched on the cement as you pulled them out. There were about a dozen sorority girls sitting by us who must have woken up hours ago to do their faces. Their skin was bronze and matte, their eyes all dark and exotic, and their bright, lightly oiled hair was pulled up into high ponytails. They were all ready for the game, wearing blue and gold beads, ribbons, and neat white sneakers. Some had gold glitter on their faces and that paint that football players put on under their eyes.

“Sometimes the idea of a thing can be more powerful and disorienting than the thing itself,” I said cryptically, pointing at Aashik’s chest with my fork. “It’s because we know that they’re part of a sorority that everyone’s acting this way.”

“What way?” He asked.

“Don’t you feel the energy’s changed, man?” I asked, motioning around. “Things are different here because of them.”

He snorted. “Eat.”

“Chakra, man. There’s something you might understand.”

When we were bussing our tables and putting our plates and cutlery into those black plastic bins, one of them, a little Asian girl in a blue tank top and torn denim shorts, came beside me to do the same. She was a cute girl, one of those girls who looked about half her age—you’d’ve dug her. I used my fork to scrape what was left on my plate, and I noticed her face sort of curl up in disgust as she looked at me do that. Okay, I thought.

We left the restaurant and began home. As we waited to cross the street, I heard shouting from behind me. It was through a brief clearing in the stream of students behind me that I saw the Asian girl in her blue tank top and her denim shorts. She was in front of Caffè Med, where all the vagabonds sit on pieces of cardboard with their gray pitbulls on chains, which lie on their chins with furrowed brows. She was trying to get herself free of the Japanese lady, who was hugging her.

“Let go!” The girl cried.

“Hey, why—” the woman said, and her arms went limp.

“Stop contacting me,” I heard the girl say quietly.

The old woman gasped and began to cry. The girl turned away, and I saw that her eyes were wide and shiny, too.

How’d the old lady fall off? I wondered as I continued to walk north on dark San Pablo. If she was Japanese, then her parents were probably interned during the war. Lost everything, had to start all over again. They wanted her to be a good student, and she probably was. But how’d she fall off like that? I guess it isn’t so much a falling off as it is an erosion. Worn down stone by stone till they were islands. My dad for example, he’s got a friend who owns a gardening supply store. He and his wife worked there all the time and made a lot of money. They’ve got a couple nice cars and a nice house. They have a son, too, who was supposed to become someone. I hung out with him a couple times. He was a year older than me and athletic. Had a stoic look, like he was a young soldier. When I was little I used to do everything really intensely, competitively. Had to win at everything and hurt bad whenever I lost. He was like that, too. This one time my family was over at his house. Big house in a gated community. Pool and tennis court in their backyard. My brother thought it was a fine occasion to put us through a decathlon. Soccer, sprinting, wrestling. We played first to ten, best of five, best of seven. This one time I remember I tried to get past him with the ball, toward the lawn chairs we used as goals, by giving him a shoulder check. Well, he hit the ground hard. Then he suddenly brought his knees to his chest and his hands to his face like I was about to hit him. When he saw me looking at him confused, he just started to cry quietly. My brother and I tried to console him, but he just cried harder and harder. Eventually we helped him up and patted his head and tried to make him laugh by pulling our shorts up to our chests and skipping around.

He’s homeless now. His parents kicked him out because he'd steal from them and buy heroin. The mom started noticing a lot of her possessions going missing, her clothes, a pearl necklace, even a leather armchair. My dad said he saw him by an on-ramp one day, lying there all burnt in the sun. The mother and father said they'd done all they could for him, and that what he did was his own business now. Man, I remember how they told my parents he’d gone to some Stanford summer camp or some shit. Now they were saying this kind of bullshit? So that's it then? You did all you could, end of story? Imagine his mother holding her newborn son in her arms and someone coming in to tell her that someday she’d kick him out of her house. I don’t know. I guess it's hard enough trying to get your own life right to worry about someone else's. And I guess it’s true that there’s a point where you just can’t reach a person anymore. I’ve never gone out of my way to really help anyone either.

Like just a couple days back, Alex had contacted me. She was going to school in Seattle now as a transfer. She told me that she had kept up with my writing during my travels, and she was wondering if I was back now. We caught up for a bit, and I asked her how she was doing. “People are nice,” she said. I remembered she said the same thing during her first week at Emerson as a freshman. We’d spent the summer after we graduated from high school talking for hours almost every day, and there’d been nothing else but the next thing she was going to say.

She told me she was having a little trouble in Seattle, and that she wasn't doing so well in school right now. She said she wasn’t okay. She'd never said that to me before. I always knew there was something going on with her. During that summer she told me that when she was little her parents and her older sister fought a lot and sometimes she’d punch the wall by her bed. She was never sad or down when we were together, but I always had the feeling that I never got to the bottom of her. Now she was telling me something important, and all I said was: "Sorry to hear that." She said she’d be alright, and we talked about something else. But afterward that made me think again about that phone call when we were eighteen. “People are nice,” she had said, “but it’s a bummer because there’s none as smart as you here.” She was always really good at saying things like that. When it was time to go, I said: "See you when I see you?" "I love you, too," she said. And I said, "I love you, too." She said she'd never said that to anyone. “So what do we do now?” I asked her. She said maybe it meant that we should always be there for each other. “Deal,” I said. I didn't think too seriously about that at the time because I thought she was gone now. And the truth is that what I’m telling you isn’t that important because it was a long time ago and she's probably forgotten all about it. But I wish I’d kept my end of the bargain.

All of a sudden I wanted to go back to that Japanese lady and wake her up and ask her what happened to her, that one Saturday morning I’d seen her trying to hug a girl, and—who was she to you? Where’s your family? But I didn't and never would. That's the thing with the homeless here. They love to talk. I figure it's because they got no one who'll listen to them. 

I continued east on University toward campus. The walking isn’t so good here because the streets are wide and a lot of the stores are out of business. There were many empty, weedy lots contained by chain link fences. The panels of sidewalk bulged over tree roots or were cracked by tremors. A blacked-out sports car ripped down the avenue toward me. As it idled at the stoplight before me, its muffled stereo made the car shudder and its licence plate rattle. I looked for a moment at its blackened windshield, trying to make out a pair of eyes inside. It drove off. I continued past the bike path that runs perpendicular to University. In the distance, two parallel streets over, I saw the orange glow of a dome tent.

About a week ago I’d spent my night walking on that bike path, a five-mile route called the Ohlone Greenway which runs along the BART line. On the grassy shoulder of the path, backed against a wooden fence, I saw a woman trying to set that tent up. Those dome tents are tricky by yourself, so I helped her set it up. She didn't look dirty or anything, she could have been my friend’s mom. She looked about fifty-five, and she had this nice, thick mane of silver hair. Anyway, she was really grateful and said I was really nice. She was an artist, she said, and pointed to her black bag, the big square ones that hold canvases. I asked her what kind of painting she did, and she went on and on about being down and out, how she was saving for a studio right now, how her boyfriend had kicked her out, how his cat had scratched her eye, and how she'd had a bad reaction. She peeled back her eyelid and showed me her eyeball. I leaned over and said "Oh, yeah." Man, she wouldn't stop. I told her I had to go and gave her a couple bucks. She gave me a big hug and said would I come visit her?

I said I would.





CHAPTER 3

THERE ARE A COUPLE CHARACTERS you see all the time around Berkeley. Like the guy Aashik and I always got a kick out of. He's an old Indian guy with an afro that's turning white at the roots. He's always wearing a dirty tan tunic and matching pants. Something about his eyes, too. There was fire in them, like he was capable of something very violent or very generous. The first couple times we saw him, up by Sproul Gate, we thought he was holding his hand out to me and saying "Asian, Asian." But later I realized he was saying "Any change, any change," which makes more sense. There's an old white guy who looks like he should be on a Roman coin. Got the white beard and the nose for it. I always see him lounging on some bench on campus, looking like he's eating grapes from the vine at a feast. He's always moving around campus with his big body bag, the kind that military guys have, only his is blue. In my head I called him Marcus Aurelius. One time I saw him reading him a paperback by those benches outside Dwinelle. I was so curious about what he was reading that I circled back around him. I got down on one knee to pretend to tie my shoe and got a look at the cover. It was one of those harlequin romances, with a Tarzan-looking fellow on the cover. With one hand he's holding a girl wearing a nightgown, and like a ship's mast with the other. And the way I was looking up at him, you couldn't have seen it otherwise, but one of his hands was down his sweatpants. After that he became Caligula.

Caligula likes to sleep on a steam vent by a gutter on Bancroft, below the tennis courts. I bet you've walked by that gutter at night and heard the rats down there going scree, scree, scree like they're fighting a rat war down there. Tonight they were still going at it, even with all the rain, so I figured I’d get a look at them once and for all. Got my phone light out and tried to get a look inside. Wet, kitten-sized rats were swarming all over the gutter floor. Rats, you know, they don't have bad faces. They're big mice. I mean, their tails might get you. Thick wormy things, like surgery scars down the belly. But what got me as I waved my light inside the gutter was the madness in their swarming. Far as I could I tell, there was no reason for it—there was no food nearby, no mating, no fighting, or predator—but every night I walked past this gutter, rain or shine, their squeals pierced out from the underground. Reminded me of that one Goya painting of Saturn munching on his kid. What gets me about that painting is how helpless his eyes look, like there’s some demon inside him calling the shots. Man, you got to have something going on in your head if you can fall asleep right by those rats going at it.

But tonight, because of the rain, he wasn’t there.

I got a little Caligula story. When I was still in school I once went to this Friday church service that my brother told me to go to. This sophomore named Nathan brought Caligula there, and he just looked confused the whole time. I remember after it was over and I was on my way out, I saw Nathan asking Caligula if he could pray for him. His eyes kind of bulged and he nodded. Nathan thanked God for their meeting and prayed for his health and stuff. I sent a silent prayer over, too, that he could knock his habit of jerking off on campus.

Nathan was nice to me. I thought he was alright. He was really upbeat, the kind of guy you don't want to be around for too long because you don't want to be sullen around him and have him ask you if you want to talk. Kind of guy who’d just look at you with a frozen smile if you made a messed up joke around him. Everything's God's will, according to him. If God wills it, he says, like he's trying to take back the Holy Land. He was a short guy with the black plastic glasses and a big smile, big front teeth I remember, and if you weren't too smart then you might think there was something saintly about him. I had told him about my travels and how I wanted to be a writer, and he went around introducing me to the other churchgoers as Tom Sawyer. This one night he hit me up and asked me if I was free. We met on the corner of Bancroft and Telegraph, right before Sproul, and the first thing he does when he sees me is he quotes a line from a story I shared with him. I remember the line, and it was a bad, self-important line. Hearing it from his mouth made me realize that. So we start walking through Sproul past the bulletin that's ten deep with fliers and past those trees that line the area. You know what trees I'm talking about? They're not too tall and in the fall they lose their leaves and their stubby branches look like knuckles. Knuckle trees, I think whenever I see them. Anyway, Nathan tells me that he and his girlfriend are going to write a book.

"About what?"

"About God.

"What about him?"

"About his goodness. About his grace, and his majesty."

Then he says the reason he wants to talk to me is because he and his girlfriend want my blessing to write their damn book.

Nathan went to my brother's church in San Francisco, like the whole Cal church group did. That was a long drive, all the way to Daly City. I remember this one sermon by this guy named Pastor Ryan. Big brutal guy, liked to wear cardigans. He had this one sermon about selflessness and all I remember about it is him talking about how these pop stars rented out a whole hospital for the birth of their baby, and that made them selfish. And about how he bought an iPad for his son who didn't like to share it, and that was also selfish but not as selfish as renting out a whole hospital and kicking out everyone on life support. He’s one of those guys who’s a better orator than a thinker, who tries to mask his bullshit with rhetoric. At the end of the sermon he tells us that when you get God, he’ll move your bowels. That made me laugh, and a couple people turned around and looked at me. At lunch I sat down by my brother and tried to talk to him about what he got out of it all. This and that, he said. I knew he'd been sleeping for most of it. Head back with his mouth open. My brother does this thing where if he doesn't want to talk he'll ignore you. You ask him something important and he'll get this dark look on his face and pretend he didn't hear you.

Reason I was going to church in the first place was that after I got back from Panama, I spent a month at my brother's. “Of course, man,” he replied when I messaged him to see if I could stay at his place. When I arrived he asked me if he could use my suitcase for when he went to New York to see his girlfriend. His was too small. I emptied the suitcase of my things. He took his things out of his suitcase and placed them in mine. This suitcase was also his. He had left it at home four years ago, and I had used it since. I looked around his small studio apartment. A two-counter kitchenette, a bathroom peeling and cracked at its edges and corners, a large TV on the wall, a desk that was being used as a shelf for his button-down shirts, slacks, and documents. His queen bed took up about half the studio. There was barely any room.

“Did you break this?” He asked me. He jerked the suitcase handle again and again, but it wouldn't go in fully. 

“I don’t know.” He kept doing it, violently. 

Christ, I thought. What did it matter?

“Did you break it?”

“I’ll fix it—I’ll fix it.”

I took the suitcase from him and set it down, putting my hand into the siding of the case to feel the metal and using the other to try to get the handle in.

“Don’t try to fix it.”

I stopped.“Just put the stuff back into the other suitcase.”

I did as he said.

“You need to get a job,” he said once I had finished and looked up.

I agreed.

He told me to take a shower. I stood under the hot water, and I remember thinking about how I wanted to stay under the water forever, how good it would be if the hot water could soften me so I could start taking myself apart under it, like peel my lips off and pluck my nose off and step out of my loose skin and have my bones crumble like sandstone in my fingers and just go into the drain so I wouldn't leave a mess for my brother. Man, I was a burden.

Only work I found was writing for a magazine. They had an opening for an internship and wanted some writing samples. So I sent in a story about my travels to the editor. It's about how I came by some money, went like this:

The road was new and black with shiny crumbs on its shoulder that crunched as you walked on them. It had bold yellow dashes that were neatly squared off on each end. The road curved through the pine forest. The air was colder and fresher here because of the elevation. Before I had gotten to the ranch I made camp in the forest and woke up to frost on the outside of my sleeping bag. But the goose down had lofted overnight, and I only felt the cold wind as a gentle tugging.

There were many monarch butterflies before me now. They were like delicate orange leaves that never fell. When I was little I read about how every year millions of them migrated to a forest near Mexico City. I thought how fantastic that forest must have been to attract them, and that it must have had magical qualities. Now I knew that I was in that place. There was a photo in the magazine of thousands of them covering a big tree. It would have been grotesque as it is with pill bugs swarming over one another on a river rock or spiders with hair-like legs stacked on top of one another in a dark basement corner. But the monarchs were beautiful in the way that they opened and closed upon the tree trunks, and the labor with which they flew, wobbly like a newborn calf. They were the color of your eyelids when you lie in the sun with your eyes closed, and around their wings they were polka-dotted with black and white like a dress a little girl might wear. To read that every year they made a great journey from all around North America made me think they were brave. It was always a special moment whenever I saw one as a child, and I was always trying to catch them because I hated to see them leave. 

A white car came around a bend and stopped for me.

“How old are you?” The woman asked me as we drove. She was clean and neat, early thirties. Her car was clean.

“Twenty-one,” I said. “And you?”

“Older than you,” she smiled.

A few minutes into the ride she placed her hand on my thigh.

“Your legs,” she said. “Do you run?”

“I used to, but not anymore.”

“It's not common to wear shorts in Mexico.”

“I know. But I'm traveling. It's more comfortable.”

“Do you have pants?”

“Yes.”

“In your backpack?”

“Yes, I think I'll change in Toluca.”

“You should change now.” She pulled over suddenly and looked at me.

“Okay.”

There was an old broken wall by the highway, and behind it was the pine forest with boughs of red-brown pine needles. It was dry and the ground was soft here and would have made a good place to camp. I went behind the wall with my jeans and put them on over my shorts. She poked her head behind the wall and walked toward me.

I held my hands up. “What are you doing?”

“I have to pee.”

We got back in the car.

“Is something wrong?” She asked me as we drove on.

“No, I'm just tired.”

Toluca 7 km, read a green sign.

“Where did you learn Spanish?”

“In school. And here in Mexico.”

“I heard Asians have small penises. Is that true?”

“I don’t know.”

“Go like this,” She said after some time, and she lifted her arms shoulder-level.

“Okay.”

She reached over slowly towards the water bottle between my legs. She placed her hand on my penis and squeezed. “Is that okay?”

“No,” I said. “No.”

“Okay.”

I told that story to a lot of people who thought it was funny, how that ride wasn't free, and was she hot and everything. Part of that story is a lie, though, and I only told Axel the truth. I did get a ride from someone who touched me. That's true. But it wasn't a girl, it was a guy. And as he drove off, he stopped for a second. I saw his hand drop a couple purple bills from the window. I went and got some tacos with that money and ate them on a big rock by the gas station. There were ants on that rock carrying green shards of leaves and I made a little game of trying to pick them up by their leaves and trading them with crumbs of beef from my taco.

The editor of the magazine called me and said I could write for them. She said I was “exceedingly talented.” Someone like her had never told me that before, and that made me feel pretty good. Started projecting way into the future about how this was the start of how I was going to be a great writer by paying my dues as a journalist first. I wrote a first draft, a self-important thing that they rejected and a second one, another self-important thing that they rejected again. Finally I wrote about meeting Aashik in Cancún during my travels. They said it was very good, and that was a big relief for me. But apart from that I couldn't bring myself to write about things that were happening in Berkeley. They did give me one other story. This was during the Ebola outbreak in West Africa. The story was getting big because a couple Americans had gotten it at airports. Apparently the robotics department at Cal and a couple other schools were teaming up to fight it. Man, consciousness just comes into your head like a sickness, like you’ve just heard some really bad news which I guess is that you’re you. Put it this way, when you haven’t eaten all day you want to bite into a juicy beef patty with some melted cheddar on top, and when you’re dead-tired you want to close the curtains of your room and fall onto the mattress of your bed. And when you do, there’s a deep satisfaction that perfectly complements the hunger or the fatigue. A perfect fit. I know you’ve felt this loneliness or this emptiness or this ennui that I’m talking about, and that the pain lies in the fact that its mate doesn’t seem to exist. No one, no thing could remedy it for you. All you can do is lie there like you’re naked on a cliff’s edge during a storm, till it goes away.

I’d lie in my sleeping bag for a lot of the day, so I’d hear things through the wall. Sometimes the clack of fingernails on the drywall, the scuff of hair, the thud of a skull. I figured our neighbor’s got her bed right up against the half-inch of drywall between us, and her head’s right by mine. It’s nice to think about. I knew it was a girl because sometimes I heard her talk on the phone with her head against the wall. I’d never seen her though—I mean, I’d probably seen her in passing outside our apartment before, but I didn’t know whose head was right by mine. And I couldn’t make out what she said exactly, only the emotion behind it, and I’d hear her laugh, too. It was a good laugh, like she could really let go and lose herself in whatever made her laugh. That’s a good quality, and people will like you for it.

Sometimes she cried, too, about once a week. She’s got a strange way of crying. You know how when soccer players get fouled, they’ll roll on the grass, grabbing their ankles and screaming? What I’ve noticed is that when the worst offenders actually get seriously hurt, they’ll just lie there, not making a sound and covering their faces, which are screwed up and far away. There’s a singularity to serious pain, where you’re taken away from everyone and everything and put deep before some abyss. Point is, there were no waterworks with this girl. She just sounded very tired and very beatdown. What she does is she makes that eerie moan that people make when they’ve had the wind knocked out of them. “Eee-eee-eee.” Then she’d sob heavily. I could feel it in the walls. Then that sound again. “Eee-eee-eee.”





CHAPTER 6

SOMETIMES I THINK I'd be better off in prison. Every Tuesday my library’s got this used book sale. Every book’s either fifty cents or a dollar, depending on if it's a paperback or a hardcover, and every time I go there I get a couple books. I figure it’s all the adult children of the old people clearing out their childhood homes when they die and donating all their books to the library. That’s my guess because every sometimes you’ll open up a nice edition of a classic and find an inscription. Other day I bought a nice hardcover poetry book, and when I opened it I saw that someone had written, in really nice handwriting that no one’s got anymore: 

Al,

Warm friends lie within these pages ...

Happy Anniversary, with love.

Connie

7.7.82

It’s sad to see, because you can kind of tell when no one’s read a book before because none of the pages are bent and the pages smell new. But I get it. Reading’s hard. I’ve got hundreds of books now, and I’ve only read a couple dozen of them. The one book I carried with me while I was travelling through Central America was The Brothers Karamazov. That’s a big book. I remember telling you that I was reading it, and you were asking me what the damn epigraph meant and who my favorite character was. Yeah, I never read that goddamn book. Ended up giving it to a Cuban named Magdial in Nicaragua. How I met that guy is a long story, so I’ll start with how I got into that country.

There are hidden trails near every border in Central America that the locals use to go between countries. I used them until I got caught about a mile into Costa Rica. A family of five in an old pick-up truck took me a few miles before the Costa Rican border which is called Peñas Blancas. I sat in the back on some plastic crates of lettuce with an old man and his two grandsons. The calm shore of Lago de Managua was only twenty feet away to our left, and it looked like it must flood the road when it rained. The gray-brown volcanoes Concepción and Maderas were on the lake's island, Ometepe. They were stout and matte and looked flat on the horizon like a myth on silkscreen. The wind took our words from our mouths so we pointed at the lake and the volcanoes and smiled. We stopped a few times to cool the engine with water. There were mango trees along the road. Each tree was forty feet high and carried hundreds of small plump yellow mangoes. There were hundreds of them on the ground, some fermenting and hairy with ants and already part of the ground, and others that were freckled, soft, and goldenrod. It was easy to kick some leaves around to find the good ones, and we returned to the truck bed with them in the bellies of our shirts. Warm syrupy juice spiraled away into the air as we sucked on the mangoes. The pulp stuck in my bottom teeth. The boys and I made a game of throwing the slimy flat stones as far as we could into the dry fields. If you threw it like you were skipping stones on a lake shore then it cut nicely through the wind before it fell curving down. The grandfather was still young with black and silver hair and smiled at us. He gave me a small, square bag of water that was cold and dripping. I tore into its plastic corner with my teeth and emptied it down my throat. He put his hand out for the plastic which he put into the front pocket of his jeans, and he smiled as I wiped my mouth.

They let me off at the border. I doubled back and turned west into the yellow grazing fields and hiked through a few miles of light and dry jungle. The roots of the trees here were dry and came off the ground like thick, dusty vines. There were a few cattle fences along the way, three or four horizontal barbed wires held up by vertical branches that I jumped over. I decided to stay west of the highway entering Costa Rica, keeping it in sight, and continued for a few miles like this. 

Soon I came across a dry riverbed There were trees whose network of thick roots was exposed like those mangroves, though there was no river here. I hopped over the riverbed and walking down its bank I found myself in a natural shelter formed by the wide trunks and roots of several large trees.

“Ey!”

I startled. A man sat against the trunk of one of these trees. He had a white LA Dodgers cap, a purple shirt, and white board shorts. He was light brown and a little overweight. His name was Charlie, he said, and he tossed his passport at my feet. It was true. He said he’d been waiting in the grove for a friend who was coming illegally from Costa Rica into Nicaragua. His friend was bringing Charlie's girlfriend. She's black, he said, so it's more dangerous for her. He said that just beyond the clearing were patrols of Nicaraguan military and Costa Rican immigration. They were staking the river that bridged the border because it was a point of territorial dispute for the two countries. They were also on the lookout for illegals. Charlie said when it got dark in an hour he would guide me part of the way and that he had crossed four or five times already. 

We sat under the tree. I told him about my travels and showed him some pictures on my camera. I told him I wanted to get to the World Cup in Brazil.

“La Mundial? Puta. There are a lot of black women in Brazil, right?”

“Probably. More than here.” I reached into my bag and tossed him a mango. His eyes brightened when he caught it, and we ate together.

We began before it was completely dark, before the hills and the trees were completely black with the night sky. Charlie walked fast, and his arms swung loosely. I hunched as I followed him and clutched my backpack straps if I had to run. The dead brush was thick and crunched as you walked. About half an hour in we saw a man in white leaning against a tree.

“Shh, shh,” Charlie said. “Inmigración.” He started walking quietly toward the man who startled up against the tree.

“Ey!”

 Charlie spun around and ran past me, and I ran after him.

“Ey! Ey!”

We ran through the brush, swishing loudly over the hollow dry stalks of grass. I felt an emptiness in my stomach and a twitching in the hollow of my temples. I looked back and saw a wild beam of light flailing into the night sky like an unleashed hose. We dropped onto our stomachs and breathed silently into the ground. Dust entered my mouth. I gathered spit in my mouth and let it drip down.

“Why did he stop?” I whispered to Charlie.

“He’s afraid,” he said. “There are robbers around here.”

We started moving again when the beam of light was gone. Charlie patted his pockets and cursed. He had lost his phone and his passport. I told him we could go back and look for them where we were lying down.

“No,” he said. “They're gone.”

We saw two more officials in white walking the opposite direction on the path to our left, about fifty feet away. Military and immigration travel in pairs, he whispered to me. I asked Charlie to take off his white cap and his white shorts. He took off his shorts and wrapped his cap in them. Then three figures carrying bags passed quickly by us on the path. They didn’t notice us.

“Ey!” Charlie whispered.

They were two men and one woman also crossing the border. They were sharp and wary as they looked at us, and they walked on quickly. We began again once they were some distance away. I wondered how long it would be before there were homes and lights and stores.

Charlie stopped. He said he was going back now because the way was clear now. I thanked him and moved on. The walking was faster, and I grew more sure with each step. The two men and women ahead of me will be discovered before me, I thought.

Two black figures suddenly appeared before me. They had the stocky builds of soldiers in uniform, and the nozzles of big guns came out of their silhouettes. We stopped and looked at each other.

“Ey! Ey!”

I ran toward the brush toward the highway, remembering that Charlie said they wouldn't shoot, that they would only shoot into the sky as a warning. My backpack bounced up and down, and I cursed.

“Ey! Ey! Ey!” 

I waited for a bullet in my back. I wondered if it would penetrate my backpack, my laptop and my clothes and all of the small things along the way. Then I was stuck. There were vines across my legs. I tried to break through them, but they were too strong. I grunted and kept pushing with only my toes on the ground. I could hear them getting closer so I bounced off the vines and fell backward on the ground. I growled and righted myself and began another way, but I tripped on a root and they were above me.

“Don't move! Don't move!” A gun was dug into my chest.

“Okay okay okay okay okay!” I said. I put my hands on the nozzle of the gun. I had never had a gun on me before. I wondered if a bullet could go through both my hands and into my body.

“Don't move!”

“Okay okay okay okay.” I lay still. “Okay.”

They pulled me up. One of them grabbed a fistful of my shirt. His nails dug into my chest. He put his light on my legs. 

“Pobrecito,” he said.

I looked down and saw many long curving cuts on my thighs. The tops of my socks were yellow and red. I looked at the vines I couldn’t break through and saw that it was a barbed cattle fence.

He put the light in my face. “De donde eres?”

“Los Estados Unidos.”

“¿Americano?”

“Si.”

He turned to the other one. “¿Americano?”

I was taken back the way I came. They asked me how much I was paid to guide the others. I told them I wasn’t guiding them. They told me to call for the others. I told them I didn’t know their names. They told me to call for the others. I told them I didn’t know their names.

They brought me to the porch of a military post. It looked like an old abandoned home with a tin roof and concrete floors. The captain there shined a light up and down my body and stopped at my legs. He whistled.

“The wire?” He asked. He was a big Spanish man with a mustache and a day’s growth of stubble. He had a masculine hairline, prominently curved at the bow of his head and receding at his temples, and the body of a fighter—thick in the neck and wrists and built for sudden bursts of power.

“Yes. Can I have some water?”

The captain called to the two men for water. They brought a plastic jug to him. He drank from it slowly.

“Ahhh,” he said when he finished, and he wiped his mouth. His forearms and his hands were combed over with patchy black hair, and his skin was bare only at the swollen joints of his fingers. He handed me the jug which had a finger’s width left. 

I shook my head.

We went inside the house which looked like an old classroom and which was empty except for a table and three or four chairs. There was a chalkboard on the wall and as I entered the room a man with a damp rag was quickly erasing in long strokes what was written on it. There were cracks in the concrete floor and yellow tufts of grass growing out of them. Mold grew on the crumbling pink walls like strong black ink through paper. The house smelled earthy and dusty from the dried feces of bats and rats. The captain sat down with his legs far apart because his gut and his thighs looked like they were going to burst. His black leather boots were laced up to their hook eyelets nearly to the middle of his calf. A black handgun was holstered to his waist.

“Why did you run?”

“I heard your men …” I didn’t know how to say “cock their guns” in Spanish so I said “chik-chik” and mimicked the action.

He frowned. He took out his walkie-talkie and asked one of the men who brought me if this was true.

He turned to me. “He says he dropped his gun. He says that's what you heard.”

“Mentiroso,” I said. “But it doesn’t matter.”

He made a note of everything in my backpack. We went through each coin’s nationality as he counted all the change I had. He was impressed with my travels. If I struck him in the head hard enough, I thought, I might be able to escape. But I’ve never struck anyone in the head before.

I was driven to a military base in the back of a convoy with another soldier. I looked down the road and thought about jumping out of the back. The soldier, who had a rifle between his knees, told me to calm down. We entered the metal gates of a military base which opened into a large concrete yard. Men and women in their baggy green fatigues walked around, and several other convoys were parked here. The Nicaraguan flag, the three stripes of blue-white-blue, stood on a pole in the center of the yard. We came out the back of the convoy and they brought me through a lobby and into a drab, dusty room. It looked like an old principal’s office. There were crates with files and an old yellowed computer turned on its side in the room. An old man with bushy gray eyebrows and mustache sat behind a desk there. His green uniform has stars, stripes, and chevrons on its epaulets and breast. He told me to sit.

“Are you efeh beh ee?”

“What?”

“Are you efeh beh ee?!”

“Efeh beh ee? Efeh beh ee,” I translated the letters in my head. “FBI? No! I'm a student,” I said. “I'm traveling!” I forced myself to laugh. I reached into my wallet for my student ID. “Look!”

He took my ID from me and moved his glasses up off his face. Then he set it down and stared at me. He asked me how much I was paid to guide the others, who they were, and how I knew the way into Costa Rica. I told him I knew nothing about that. He motioned for me to put my backpack on the table and searched through it and took photos of everything. He had problems working the flash.

“You don’t want flash,” I said.

“Sit,” he said.

He went through my notebook and took photos of some pages. I sat up in my seat to see which ones.

“Your diary?”

“Yes.”

“Are you worried I'm taking pictures of it?”

“It's personal.”

“You write a lot?”

I shrugged.

He shook my empty backpack onto the table. Bits of paper, dirt, a plastic spoon, and some pens fell out. He took photos of my cuts and mugshots of my profiles. Then he sent me into another room where a medic looked at my cuts. There were about a dozen in total, curving and perforated. They were coagulating into a brown paste and were stiffening now. I knew they were there when I moved, but there was not very much pain because they were shallow. She used a tongue depressor to spread a white cream over my cuts. It stung like she was moving the tips of grass blades over them, and I wanted to itch between the cuts. She dipped the same depressor again and again into the jar and there were flakes of blood and dirt in it when she was finished.

We drove to the border immigration office a few miles north. They went through my backpack and made a note of everything that was inside. They sent me to sit on a bench in the lobby. Some of the younger ones came over and took photos of my thighs with their flip phones.

“Great,” I said. “Great.”

I would be taken to Managua in the morning. They took me to a cell in the back of the building. There was a cot for the guard outside it. Within were two bunk beds with thin blue mattresses. There was a woman who was white with curly hair and rectangular glasses. She asked me through the bars what had happened. I told her. She told me to put my hands through the barred window on the door. She held them in hers gently and examined the cuts on them and asked me more questions. I was too tired for that. Another official came in and told me to give him his hands. He handcuffed me. Orders, he told me. I asked him if he would give me a book out of my backpack. I have three, I said. No.

There were messages and tags from other illegals written on the white cinder block walls. There were names with dates and Bible verses. One of them was only a few days old:

Trust in the Lord with all your heart

and lean not on your own understanding.

In all your ways submit to him,

and he will make your paths straight.

- James, Ghana

I lay on one of the bunks with my cuffed hands on my chest and my knees up so the blood and cream wouldn’t get on the mattress. I felt the metal bars of the bunk under me. I got to thinking about how great it would be if I could stop time. In the morning I’d uncuff myself. Then I’d find my passport and stamp it twice for entry and exit. I’d walk south, take a car and drive for a few days. Race down the aisles of grocery stores in shopping carts and eat whatever I wanted. But if time was stopped then wouldn’t air molecules be suspended? So how could I breathe? Did matter beyond my body keep their properties when time was stopped? Was water still wet, was the sun still hot, did air molecules vibrate to make sound? Would I be able to swim or would the ocean be as solid as ice? It shouldn’t be possible to disturb matter when time was stopped. Otherwise the matter I disturbed would disturb other matter which would disturb other matter. Did time stop for my organs? Would food go bad? No, the food would never go bad. It would never be hard or impossible to move. I’d spend a week on the beach, find a room in a great hotel and eat anything I wanted. The water would be normal moving water so I could swim. The mosquitoes would leave me alone. I would practice riding a motorcycle for a few days, I would learn how to fly a small plane and take it down to Rio. Axel would be there—he was studying abroad in Santiago and we’d talked about meeting up to see a game together. We’d have a good time partying and making new friends and meeting girls. But why stop there? I could turn myself around back to San Diego and ask for a trial with a semi-pro team. I’d slow down time as much as I needed to make sure that I was the best and fastest player on the pitch. Then I’d work my way up to the national team and come June I’d be adjusting my armband and walking on cleated feet out the tunnel to the roar of 80,000 Brazilians.





CHAPTER 7

There was Magdial from Cuba, Adnan from Syria, James from Ghana, and Chris from Oklahoma, who was the only other American. I can’t remember the names of any of the other men, though I do remember their faces. You need a name to anchor a face. Without it, a face eventually blurs into a soft, ethnic composite. In total there were forty men in the detention center from Bulgaria, Ethiopia, Ghana, Somalia, Syria, Bangladesh, Pakistan, India, and Nepal. They were refugees who were making their way up to America by seeking asylum in each country along the way.

 Of the forty I was the only one traveling south. Everyone but Oklahoman Chris was going north to America. He was a short and portly man, wearing a faded shirt that was big for him and with a big hole in its belly. There was something motherly about him, and he said it was good to meet an American for a change. He had been taken in by the police because he was drunk in the streets of Honduras, having no papers, had been brought here. He said he had a Honduran wife and kids and a hog farm. I asked him what was going to happen to him. He was sad when he said that he would probably have to leave his family and go back to America.

The center was a plain single-story building, almost like a library you’d find in a small town in the midwest. You entered through the shabby offices of government workers and out into a courtyard where the men sat. I hiked up my shorts to expose my cut thighs when I entered. Everyone stared at me and then at my legs. Across the yard was a large room with about twenty-five bunk beds and a bathroom. There were tiled walls that separated the toilets and showers. The toilets were holes in the ground, and the showers were small pipes about chest-high that dribbled water.

Countrymen stayed together, and they took turns playing ping-pong in the courtyard. The ball had a hairline fracture and bounced dead sometimes. Indians played cards in the yard, Bangladeshis puffed smoke out the barbed-wire fence toward an empty lot. Magdial, Adnan, Chris and I spent our time together. Magdial was a stocky Spanish-looking young man, only a little older than me. He was friendly and his voice often took on a high pitch when he was excited. Adnan was a tall and serious man with a shaved head who was in his mid-thirties. He was an accountant who’d left Mosul before Syria’s civil war a few months ago. For six months, he and Magdial, having flown into Brazil from Havana and Mosul, had made their way north. With a local guide they had hired, he and a group of other refugees crossed the Darien Gap on foot. 

Once during a river crossing with only a line of rope from bank to bank, an old man had nearly drowned. Just go yourself, he told Magdial. But Magdial managed to drag him through and they lay on the bank.

“It was our lives, man! Our lives! Fuck! For me, this isn’t traveling. For me this is life and death.”

When he asked me why I hadn’t just crossed legally I didn’t have an answer. As an American I had to pay a fine of $80. Aashik wired me the money immediately and another $70 on top of that. Some of the other men would not be processed for weeks. A tall Bulgarian man with a severe nose told me he had been caught at the border and sent to prison for six months before he arrived at the detention center. He showed me the bedsores on his sides from having to sleep on the bare floor.

James from Ghana slept across from me. He was always lying in his bed and looking up at the bunk above him. Whenever he walked by me I’d give him a nod. He’d just look at me. I thought he was just being an ass. One day saw him kneeling by his bed on the second day, and when he was done and had dusted his knees I asked him if he was praying. Yes, he said. I told him I had seen the verse he had written in the bunk room, and we talked. He spread peanut butter on a slice of bread and gave it to me. The inmates were allowed to pay the immigration officials to buy food for them. It was with Adnan his third week in the detention center.

“In my country this is jail,” he said. “In my country I am a good man. For me this is jail. I have never even been to jail, but this is jail for me.”

Some of his friends and extended family were in New York. He had seen photos of them online about how they lived now. He said he wouldn’t live like them. He said he would save up all his money and send it back to his wife and children.

“But she plays games with me, man. She plays games with me.”

Then the guy started to cry. I didn’t know what to do. All I could do was look at him.

There was one woman whose name I never learned. She slept alone in the female quarters. She had a mohawk, not the spiky kind, but a thick strip that ended in a mullet. On the whiteboard in the yard she drew fake Chinese symbols, skull-and-crossbones, and the word “revolucion” over and over. At night we heard her howl and curse in Spanish. Some of the men shouted back. Shut the fuck up, crazy bitch. But no one knew why she was here, and we were afraid of her.

In the yard we played rummy and ate lunch. It was always some form of rice and beans on a plastic plate, which the men grumbled about, but I stayed quiet. It was better than I had been eating the past couple months, and we got it three times a day. The woman approached me and said that she was a doctor who could treat the cuts on my thighs. She placed her hands on my calves. I told her I was okay.

“Don't let her touch you, dude,” Magdial whispered to me. “It was drugs. Drugs made her into a crazy bitch.”

Magdial told me that when he first saw my legs he thought I had been tortured, that I was Yakuza, which made me laugh. Now the white cream had attracted gray dust and combined with my browned blood into a dry gravy that flaked off my thighs. Adnan and Magdial told me I needed to see a doctor, but I knew it wasn’t serious. There were many cuts, but none of them were deep or swollen. I carefully rinsed them in the dribbling shower each day, and in the afternoon I lay in the sun to dry them.

There was a small television in the room. The men were fixed on it. They stared at the pop stars dispatching villains in their music videos, celebrities sharing self-effacing anecdotes to adoring hosts and audiences, chefs adding handsome vegetables to blenders.

I tried to read a little. They wouldn’t let us bring our things in, but they let me bring in a book when I flipped through the pages for them and showed them there was nothing inside. I got in my bunk and lay there trying to read the first page. Magdial had told me to pile two of the thin mattresses on the bunk so the metal bars wouldn’t dig into you. Once he came over on his way to the bathroom and looked at the cover. 

“The Brothers Karamazov?” He said. He said it like a real Russian would.

“Yeah.”

He was really excited and said it was his favorite book and that he’d been meaning to read it again.

“There’s so much soul in him, man. If you want soul, all you need is Dostoesvky,” he said. “‘God and the devil rage, man, and their battlefield is the heart of man, man.’”

It was my last day in the center, and I figured I’d never get around to reading the book anyway, so I gave it to him. He was really happy, like I’d just given him a hundred bucks. One of the Bangladeshis was lying in bed with a cigarette. I offered him some change for one, but he said it was okay. We smoked as we lay in our bunks. There were no more good stories to tell one another. The ceiling fan clicked and took the smoke away. There were the squeals of our droopy spring mattresses, the soft pound of chalky feet on the blasted concrete, the traffic beyond, and the clean metronomic clucks of ping pong in the yard. Murmurs in Spanish, Hindi, Arabic, and Somali blended low. I wrote and rewrote lists and drew and redrew calendars of what I was going to do with the next two years of my life. In the summer I would go to Alaska where my friend John was. I had met him when I worked during the winter at a ranch in northern California. I would walk the docks every morning and ask for a job on a salmon boat. If I couldn’t get one I would gut fish in a cannery. When I had enough money I’d go buy land in a forest in Montana or Idaho. I’d build a small cabin near a snow-fed stream I could use to cook and drink. I’d get the right tools and learn how to use them. I’d get a book on the local flora and fauna so I could forage for berries and mushrooms, and I’d fish and hunt small game and cook it on a wood stove. The stove would warm the cabin. I would bring all my books and make a big bookshelf for them. It’d be painful and I’d probably go crazy, but maybe it’d let me get a grip.





CHAPTER 8

THERE WAS A BLACKOUT on campus that night. Reason was, someone had stolen copper wire from some place outside campus, and that had somehow caused an explosion on campus. It came out of one of those steam vents. A man-sized fireball, I heard. I thought about all those rats going scree, scree, scree as they were roasted alive. I looked it up and apparently the price of copper has been going up over the past couple years and you could get a lot for that wire. But what really interested me was the guy who orchestrated it all. I mean, who do you know who’s got the brains to monitor copper prices and find out where there’s a lot of it and got the balls to break the law like that? That’s a guy who likes life.

I figured it was a good night to walk around campus because there would be nobody around and it’d be pitch black. I was wearing the same thing I was wearing the night before when I helped John out, just a hoodie and some jeans. I walked out of the apartment and came out onto the street. Parker St, where you used to live, too. The air was cold off the damp ground, and it was a good sight to see the beginnings of bright green sprouts out of the unkept lawns of the houses nearby. It looked like the rain had gone for good, and where the cement was now beginning to dry looked stained by oil. I walked out on Telegraph. For once the Campanile wasn’t lit up. I passed by that one izakaya Japanese joint where they’ll serve you all kinds of food and drinks for hours. They’re open late, and when all the guests have gone, all the employees hang out over some tea or spirits inside there. I’ve never set foot inside, but it’s got that old Japanese feel to it, all cramped and cozy with dim lighting, like it would have been the local tavern when the Portuguese missionaries landed there. Naturally a place like that attracted a lot of young hip types, who’d have to wait outside under heat lamps. It’s not bad walking on Telegraph. There’s still a lot of life left on it, and it’s good to be around good food, cafes, and young students hurrying home or young couples holding hands with that brightness and generosity in their eyes. I like looking at young couples. For one thing, they know that right where they are is where they should be. Sometimes I find myself wishing I were 18 again so I could do things the right way. I hadn’t stepped far out enough into the world to do anything with what I had then.

I passed by the red-and-white striped awning of Moe’s with its four stories like a big candy store. There weren’t many people inside. A funny clarinet piece was playing over the speakers inside. It was one of those pieces of music that you could play to show how serious a part music had in your life. I figure it’s only a matter of time before Moe’s is gone, like Caffe Med across the street is now. If you remember, Moe’s is where you sold my copy of Notes from the Underground because you needed laundry money. That story gets funnier and funnier the older I get.

I got to thinking about what it’s like in those first moments of meeting someone who turns out to be a very good friend. I think it’s like you’ve been walking through a thick forest your whole life. In the beginning you might have started off on the wide trail with a whole band, your brother and sister, your parents, and your childhood friends. But you lose them along the way and go off on your own, following old overgrown trails that people have left behind. Sometimes you follow these trails looking for something and you find a dead guy, and I guess that’s what it feels like to read something true that was written a long time ago, one of those things you’ve known all along but never had the words for. Sometimes you meet some people in some strange places, but it doesn’t take long to figure out that you’re looking for different things. Sometimes it’s nice to just talk to someone, but you know it can’t go on for too long. And it can get thorny if that someone is a pretty girl. But most of the time it’s a lonely walk, and it can really wear you down a little every day. But then there are those rare times, that you’ll get maybe three or four times in your life, where you haven’t seen a soul for days and suddenly you come across someone sweating and glowing alongside you.

I remember I first met you at a concert on campus just before I left for Mexico. Alan brought you along, and we passed around 40s and joints. You were a good-looking man with a head of curls. You were built like a linebacker, but you had the eyes of someone who’s had his fair share of pain. You kept your hands in your jacket pockets and looked around a lot. You didn’t really move to the music, but neither did I. After the concert we found out we lived on the same street so we walked back home together. You said you liked Russian literature like Dostoevsky and Tolstoy, and you were studying philosophy. At that time I didn’t think very much of philosophy because I didn’t know much about it. I asked you if you wanted to be a writer. Yes, you said carefully. I told you I wanted to be one, too. We didn’t get to know each other too well before I left for Central America, but there was the understanding that when the time was right we would be good friends.

Well, I was in Central America for six months, and after you graduated you went to Korea to teach English. I think we were both lonely for good company, and I was glad I had someone to talk to during that time. I wrote about my travels online, and you were about the only person who gave me your thoughts on it. Like I said, you probably didn’t know this then, but writing was the only thing I had going for me at that time.

Anyway, I got up to Sproul, and I could already feel something different about the place. Maybe you’ve felt the same when the electricity’s out at your place, and I don’t even know if there’s any science behind it, but when there’s no electricity, or maybe when you know there’s no electricity, things feel different. Feels calm, really calm, all the way to the bottom of your lungs like when you take a long drag. I wasn’t sure if it was because that low hum that’s always around when you’re in the grid was gone, or if it was because the entirety of campus was in blackness. The Campanile, which you can always see from across the Bay was unlit for once, all of the lights that glow up plants and trees were gone, and there was no one around. It was like you’d entered a quiet forest.

When you don’t have electricity for a long time, you change. We chatted once about this, how all our automation was turning us into automatons. Take two different approaches to making a cup of tea, for example. The modern way is to use a tap to fill an electric kettle, turn it on, wait for it to boil, and plop a tea bag inside. The primitive way would be to light a fire for which you needed to have gathered kindling, stoke it to create an even flame, gather water from a stream for water, strip a few leaves from a plant, etc. The manual nature of primitive tasks makes you engage with the world—the hot fire, the cold water, the scented leaves—and it’s this physical engagement that grounds you in the reality of who you are. You aren’t a king with a servant for everything, and you aren’t a god who’s impervious to fire and breathing smoke or catching a cold if you fall into the stream. You’re an animal man. I had a psych professor who in our discussion of bias said that the mind always looks for the most cognitively lazy way out. What I think he meant is that there’s a part of us that wants to turn ourselves into automatons, to nestle ourselves in kingly physical comfort so that we can nestle also in delusion. But as you know, the comforts of the body are at odds with the comforts of the soul.

During our chat you mentioned Jung’s Bollingen Tower. I found this in his writings:

At Bollingen I am in the midst of my true life, I am most deeply myself … I have done without electricity, and tend the fireplace and stove myself. Evenings, I light the old lamps. There is no running water, and I pump the water from the well. I chop the wood and cook the food. These simple acts make man simple; and how difficult it is to be simple!

I want to tell you about a time I felt that. It was towards the end of my journey, sometime in April, when I worked on an island in Panama. One day the men left for mainland for some tools, and I stayed behind. I felt that excitement like when you have a big house to yourself. That night I made a fire on the east side of the island near the stucco frame house, which the men were making into a surfers’ hostel. I set up my pad by it and lay down. I wrote a little by the fire and listened to the waves below. They rasped when the tide sucked them in and sprayed upon the rock walls of the cliff. When I was little before I went to sleep I’d have these images of being on a lifeboat on the slope of a wave that was gathering itself hundreds of feet into the air. The thought of waiting to die like that terrified me and I’d lay pinned to my bed.

I felt something touch my sleeping bag. It was a blue crab about the size of my hand. It had those beady snail eyes. I got a saucepan from the house and trapped the crab beneath it. Then I used a spatula to scrape the crab into the pan. I poured oil on the pan and held it over the fire. The oil began to spit out of the pan, and the crab became a violent shiny red with blue-gray still on its edges and legs. I broke it apart with a rock and pulled out the soft white meat. It was sweet, salty, moist, and good because you had to work for it and because there was so little.

It was a small island on the Pacific coast of Panama. You could make a loop around the whole thing in an hour. It rose about twenty feet from the ocean, and a strip of white beach framed it like a moat. It was covered in dry forest, except where it had been cleared, and was somewhere between tropical and Mediterranean in vegetation and climate. It had the laziness of coastal Spain or Greece in its dryness and sunlight, but in May the rain began, and every day we had an afternoon shower. There were two dark and big avocado trees in the center of the island, close to the main hut. They were at least thirty feet high, and their leaves were purple and leathery. Hundreds of avocados in a tree like black jewels, big avocados like the ones they have in supermarkets. One of the trees bore fruit with darker, purple-green shells and it was creamier and softer than the fruit of the other tree, whose shells were smooth and green like zucchini and whose fruit was unripe. Scattered around the island were also four or five papaya trees. The trees were thin and only had a few leaves, and if you gave them time they swelled with a single melon-sized fruit that looked too heavy for its thin trunk, like a slender woman who’s big with child. They were unripe if they were green and the same powdery color as their big star-shaped leaves, but when they became orange or yellow you tapped the fruit with a long pole so that it swayed and the weak stem gave. You had to catch the melon in your shirt and bring it quickly away from you to cradle it like a lacrosse stick or it would burst if it hit the ground. When you split the fruit there was the faint smell of bile, and its round black seeds gave you the feeling that they were swarming insects. But after you scraped them off you had the soft pink-red fruit whose belly was serrated and crystalline with sugar. The fruit was soft enough that it fell apart between your tongue and the roof of your mouth and went down like a sweet liquid. There was also an array of pineapple bushes, but they were too young to bear fruit, and there was a plantain grove whose trees were like stalks and under whose palm fronds were the green or yellow tooth-like rows of bananas. But they were thicker than bananas and tasted like their tart, dry ends until you fried them with oil and sugar. Then they softened and were filling like meat.

In the forest the leaves covered the ground so thickly that your feet sank to the shin like you were stepping into snow. When you cleared them away the bottommost leaves were disintegrated, only gray stems and veins like a fish plucked clean. The soil was black and moist like spent coffee grounds and every fistful of earth flashed and squirmed with insects in your palm. There were no animals larger than chickens on the island, no snakes, pigs, squirrels—I don’t ever recall even seeing a rodent. I guess you’d need to introduce them to the island, or they’d have to stowaway for them to be there. It’s nice to think about the island coming out of the ocean as cooling magma, then the first bird dropping seeds on it, and now it was covered with life and deep with it, too. And everything growing out of the remains of everything that died on it. I wondered how it was possible for the island to get more and more life and matter, and I figured it must be the work of millions of small birds. Seeds in their poop, insects for their young, fish and crabs washed up in the stink of low tide, rain every afternoon, sunlight—a handful of life arriving to the island every day for hundreds of thousands of years.

A stout, dark-skinned man named Tony owned the island. One of his calves was heavily scarred, nearly carved out. It was hairless from the scarring, knotted and shiny like a piece of polished wood as he dragged it behind him. But his eyes were kind, black and glittering like you always had an inside joke with him. He and two brothers were slowly transforming the island into a surfers’ hostel. I worked for six weeks under another man, an American named Richard, a tan and wiry man of 62 years with a dark hairy chest. Some part of his face or one of his limbs was always twitching, and he usually wore a scowl. So it was five men on that island, including me.

Every morning I got the same frayed rice sack from the main hut and went around the island picking fruit and collecting eggs. The hut was a primitive island structure, thatched and roofed with king palm fronds that were dry and yellow-gray with age. Inside were four bunks with green mosquito nettings, a chicken coop, and a long wooden counter overlooking the ocean, which served as our kitchen. There were some groceries from the mainland like vegetables, pasta, rice, cooking oil, and spices, and we cooked on a two-burner stove that was attached by a hose to a squat propane tank. We cooked with and drank from the rainwater that collected into a blue plastic garbage bin from the rain gutter of the main hut. The water was tart and slightly metallic. I drank it liberally. The two brothers fished often and would salt the perch and dry it out on the rock wall in front of the hut. The small cubes of rock salt looked tasty on the tan flesh that had in the sun shrunken beneath the spine and rib bones. The brothers spoke a quiet, mumbling Spanish that was incoherent to me and kept to themselves.

The south side of the island had dozens of coconut trees. You could go around picking up coconuts and shaking them to see if they had juice. The glossy green ones sloshed with more juice than the grey-brown ones, but the latter were sweeter. I got good at that two-part machete strike that would create a wedge into the shell for me to drink its juice. I’d shake the juice out into a bowl, then I split the shell with an overhead strike and carved out the hard white meat with a spoon. Ants swarmed under me and bit my bare feet as juice and slivers of fruit ran down my front, and I would rub my feet over each other to lessen the sting.

To wash I would go into the ocean and rub sand into my skin and hair. The water was warm and shallow, the clear aqua that flashes with sunlight and means paradise. Then I would walk back up the carved stairs to the hut and rinse the salt off my skin with bowls of rainwater from the blue bin. 

Sometimes I went for a swim in the ocean. You could go far out because on the north side of the island where the skiff was moored, the waves were waist-high. But I wouldn’t go too far. The shallowness continued for some length before it dropped off suddenly into a wasteland of powdery-gray detritus and narrow brown fish that picked at it and which darted when your shadow was on them. The gray pillowy floor was bare except for the fish, rocks, and brown seaweed about a foot high. The floor had a strange consistency, soft like river silt, but airy so that you could dig your hand deep into it, though it took me a lot of courage to do that. The sunlight was geometric here and cut cleanly toward the floor like prisms from the surface, which here were the heavens themselves. At this depth the weight of the ocean pressed on you and gave you the sharp pain in your ears which you squeezed out by holding your nose and snorting. The fear was there too, the prey feeling. Darkened the mind and the edges of your vision and you think there was a shark or a barracuda coming at you in your blind spots.

Tony had me restore the walkway to the frond-thatched hut, to dust and remove detritus from the stone-carved staircase up the cliff. There was a mound of about 100 clear glass bottles of Panamanian rum in the woods, and he wanted me to plant them bottom-up on the path between the hut and the hostel which was at the time only the two-bedroom stucco frame. The hostel was a half-mile away, on the other side of the island. After soaking them in soapy water I scraped off their labels with steel wool and gouged out the earth with a spade to plant them. Sometimes I took things like a generator or a small refrigerator by wheelbarrow between the hut and the hostel. The men were older than me and got tired quickly. They were digging a well on the east end of the island where the hostel was.

Tony had a boy who had come with us on the island on my first time. We’d picked him up from school and drove through the night to the beach where the skiff was moored. We drove in a pick-up truck with three seats in the front, and the boy whose name wasn’t Spanish but something long and indigenous, was sullen with me. Then some time later he decided that he liked me and he would shout my name whenever he arrived on the island. He would strip down to his white underwear and would stay like that until he left the island again He followed Tony or me around and would help me with my chores. There was a battered and flat ball in the hut, and we kicked it around. Sometimes when I walked the edge of the cliff I saw him swimming in the ocean. He showed me with a knife how to peel the green skin off the sugar cane and and bite it with the molars to release the juice and to suck on it until only the stringy pulp remained. He was confident with the knife and the machete that he brought down in one clean motion to cleave the cane off so that it was like an organ pipe, and he smiled as he sucked on his end of the cane. He was good and happy and he loved his father, and I avoided him.

Richard helped me put together a shelter on my first week on the island, on the cliff of one of the eastern points of the island. We carried a long bamboo rod to the site where it acted as a tarp pole. He let me borrow his tent, which I patched with duct tape. Richard had plotted out some land on the south side of the island where he was going to build a home. I offered to help him with it. We spent a few hours that day beachcombing for sea glass, uncracked shells, and polished pieces of driftwood. There were sizzling tufts of yellow sea foam and wobbly knobs of jellyfish pierced with sunlight. There were tire shavings, bottles of plastic and polished glass. The glass was dark amber, medicine blue, clear, and frosted, and there were the frayed remains of webbed lawn chairs, shoes, blue netting, aluminum cans, and rusty pieces of metal.

Richard and I met every half-hour to show each other what we had found. We spent most of our time at a distance with our attention at our feet and our eyes searching for any strange and man-made colors, shapes, and patterns. He was looking for the other mate of a pair of sandals. I returned all that I found to the sand in a small pile and looked with him. I realized that we wouldn’t find pairs of any kind on this beach. But eventually I found an off-brand sandal for him that was the same size. We walked ankle-deep toward a smaller beach that could only be reached in the low tide. He said that everything we needed could be found on the shores of remote islands near America and that we didn’t need to make anything anymore, except for food.

“Who did that to you?” Richard asked me, clapping a pair of tongs at my thighs. “Or what?”

We were back at the main hut. He was frying some chopped plantains, and I was flouring some fish to deep-fry. The two brothers had brought back a lingcod and two rockfish from the rockier east-end of the island and filleted them, too. I cut the flesh into small, six-inch pieces. Then I patted them with some flour and dipped them into a bowl of whisked eggs. A saucepan of vegetable oil was heating on the burner beside the plantains, which were sizzling now and smelling sweet. The two brothers had returned from their fishing and were smoking quietly on the cliff before us. Tony was barefoot, hacking at sugar cane with a rusty machete just outside the hut. I told Richard what happened.

“Let me see that shit up close,” he said, and I hiked up my shorts with my powdered hands. The scars were white and spidery against my darkened legs. They looked like I had done them to myself, and I remember that’s what you thought on our way to Joshua Tree.

“Motherfucker,” he said. “Good for you.” 

“Check out my moon,” he said. He pulled up his shirt from the back. His back was dark, freckled, and wispy with blonde hair. There was a white hairless crescent about the width of three fingers below his shoulder blade. “My old man gouged me out with one of them jumbo Jenga blocks one day.”

“Why?”

“Long story, my fault.” He took a wrinkled, hand-rolled cigarette out of his pocket and lit the tobacco fibers poking out of it on the gas stove. It flared like a fuse toward the barrel of the cigarette. He took a long drag until the crimped tobacco glowed and snapped. “Mostly,” he said through a mouth of smoke. He offered me his pouch of tobacco, but I shook my head. “I did a lot of martial arts when I was younger. Taekwondo, kung fu, jiu jitsu. Bruce Lee was my hero, of course. I respect Asians ‘cus they’re always about the hard way, the mental way. Maybe you got that in you, too. Have you seen that Tom Cruise picture, Last Samurai?”

“Yeah.”

“You remember that scene when they’re training?”

“‘No mind.’”

“Yeah man! Exactly.” He banged the oily tongs on the wooden counter. “‘No mind.’ ‘No mind.’

“One day while my dad was out, my mom was giving me flak for something I didn’t do, some chore. I was coming up, you know, getting bigger and stronger every day. My dad was a big fitness nerd, former gymnast. When we lived in the San Juans he’d do Imogene Pass every morning, rain or shine. That’s about a mile-gain in elevation, hombre. He used to walk around our house on his hands like some master sensei. Went through a fuckton, too. Grew up in the middle of dust bowl Oklahoma. Parents dead before he was 15. Car crash. The fuck, right? Raised his sisters and brothers himself. Great Depression, all that. Killed a couple Krauts. Not D-day, but I think he rode into Berlin, smoking a cigar through the tank hatch. Might have liberated some stick-figure Jews, too. Fuck, man. That’ll make or break you. Or both, like what happened with my dad.”

The oil in the saucepan was bubbling now, and I used the tongs to lower the fish into it. The oil turned white and fizzled like poured soda, but it calmed in a few minutes. The fish became crip and golden-brown like fried chicken and filled the hut with a good smell. Tony poked his head in and smiled. I laughed to myself about how he looked with his grin and his machete in his hand.

“Lingcod!” Richard shouted. “Get your lingcod!”

We took our plates with generous helpings of plantain, fish, and avocado and brought them over to the cliff beneath which were the steps to the beach. The heat of the day was giving way to the rain that came around five o’clock every day now. The rain was warm and pleasant to walk around in. A few afternoons I’d taken a swim in the ocean as it stormed and sat in the shallows to watch the murky gray-green horizon crack. A few days ago we tried to take the skiff to the mainland with two Americans who were there to surf. The gray ocean rolled us up and down as lightning grew down into the horizon like fine white roots. Richard and two Americans who were there to surf were under the blue canvas hood of the skiff. I sat shirtless at the bow. Richard offered me a jacket. I told him I was okay. The raindrops were many small slaps on my back. Like mosquitoes, I thought, but less needle-like. The boat crashed into the faces of waves and pointed skyward. The waves washed over us, and I licked my lips for the warm salt. I figured it wasn’t a bad way to go and thought there was real beauty to our smallness within the ocean and the waves dimpled by the rain. Behind me they were shouting about gas and which way was north. Richard was going, “Damn, had to be the ocean, huh?” He put a hand on my shoulder and shouted in my ear to look for land. I nodded. I was in their hands now and we were in the hands of the ocean. Then the island appeared before us as a dark forested mound, and they laughed and clapped me on the back.

“So my ma,” he continued, a limp piece of steaming, flaked fish in his hand, “she’s in my room and giving me shit and I’m giving it back, and she pushes me, catches me off-balance. It was almost instinct, man—but as I’m falling backward, I kick up with my legs, kicked her right in the chest, and I remember the look of surprise on her face as she fell. Man. I ran away from home because I was so scared of what I did. Ended up calling my parents by the Greyhound to tell ‘em I was sorry. I’ll never forget what my dad said. He said: “There’s no going back from what you’ve done.” Scariest walk back home I’ve ever had. Thought about hitchhiking and leaving my parents forever. Maybe I coulda gotten a headstart on building this house on this island. But yeah, my dad beat the fuck out of me when I got back home.”

“With the Jenga block?”

“Oh. Yeah. No, that was actually a different time, when I pissed him off for acting out in school or something like that. He threw that shit at me as I was turning my back on him. Ripped a fucking hole in my shirt. Thought I got shot.

“My daddy beat me. A lot. Man. When you get hit you start to lie. You lie because look where being yourself got you. All shriveled up at the foot of your father. So you're like, ‘this ain't me, this ain't me,’ and you lie to yourself about who you are and pretty soon you believe it. But inside you know, and you hate yourself for it.

“When it came time to get beat I’d burst into tears and fucking go sulk for days afterward. It’s not the pain, right? It’s the idea that you could be the cause of so much hatred for your dad. Any love you got for the old man goes back to zero. Then it starts up again and wham. Zero. So why bother in the first place? I wanted to beat him by pretending to feel nothing, even to like it a little. You think if you don’t retaliate you’re due some credit for when you’re older. And let me tell you, my man—I’ve cashed out. Here I am, alone on this island. Wife’s afuera!” He backhanded the hair. “Kids are God-knows-where.

“I'll tell you how much I hated that motherfucker and my mama too for being weak in front of him. One day we were over at a friends'. I was wrestling around with the kids in the backyard. Rich fuckers, had a tennis court and a water slide for their goddamn pool. My mom comes in to tell us that daddy’s sick, so we followed her up to the master bedroom. My dad's lying in their bed, looking yellow as I'd ever seen him. Turned out to be a tumor. But you know what I remember is seeing him all fucked up was one of my happiest memories with him. I crawled up onto the bed and fluffed his pillow and pulled the covers over him I was so happy. What a nice son you’ve raised, the other mom said. Felt light, like I wouldn't have to walk on eggshells around him anymore the fucking bastard. I mean, what does a man get from conquering his kid? Every night before I went to sleep, the hate burning my eyeballs like acid, I told myself that one day I’d leave and never speak to them again. Then they’d wonder if I’d still be around if they’d given me a fucking speck of decency.” 

He laughed suddenly. “So here I am.”

The latter half of that April I spent shirtless and became as brown as the other men. Mosquito bites came and went on my back. At night after dinner and after the mosquitoes had left the stillness of the sunset I wrote and read by candlelight while the men rolled cigarettes and drank by the main hut. One night I was writing inside my tent. Sometimes the mosquitoes were escapable if you didn’t netting. They weren’t there where you were, but eventually they all figured out where you were. During the first half of my trip, all the way down the tail of Mexico, my arms and ankles were covered with mosquito bites. At night the heat of my sleeping bag would radiate onto my bites and activate them. Half-asleep and half-crazed in the ecstasy of it I would itch and itch, and in the morning my fingernails would be stuffed black and smell metallic. But here I was glad to finally have a home and be inside the mesh where the mosquitoes couldn’t get to me and a tarp to keep me dry.

I had cut the top off a plastic soda bottle and put a candle inside. I used some of its hot wax to hold it in place.

Richard came by my tent and sat cross-legged in front of me. I saw through the mesh that he had a bottle of that white rum.

“Hey man,” he said. “If you want we could build you a house, too. Right here. 12 by 12 by ... 10, or 12. Whatever you want. When the surfer hostel gets going we can go to mainland and advertise, bring ‘em back here and get some commission ... I’m thinking a lofted bed, could get you a propane tank and a stove. Make your morning coffee and look out at that.” He gestured to the ocean. “Think about it, think about it. La Isla de what?” He smiled big. “De Afuera!” And he backhanded the air. “Afuera!”

I laughed and told him we could get started soon. He wanted to talk more, but I told him I was going to bed. He left, and I lay in my sleeping bag for some time. I thought about Alex, as I usually did before I went to sleep.

I’d had on my computer a video of Alex and me on a beach in Fort Bragg, California. We spent a weekend there at a bed and breakfast after I had worked there during the winter break of my sophomore year. One morning we walked a few miles along the sea cliffs. We crossed a long wooden bridge between two cliffs which lay over a shallow murky lake that carried into the ocean. There was a policeman wading slowly toward something under the bridge, and we leaned over the rail to see what it was. It was a dead man floating on his back.

The beaches of Northern California are not like the ones in Southern California. They’re narrow and above them are wet and rocky cliffs and rock formations you climb down to get to the beaches. On the cliffs are meadows of green grass and yellow and white wildflowers, and there are also tall, skinny trees that are bedheaded from the cold sea winds and gesture inland. The water is so cold that it feels like it’s bruising your bones from the inside. The waves have bored holes in some of the outcroppings of rock and have made arches and islands. Some of the islands are flat and have fresh grass and wildflowers upon them. At first you think there’s also trash on these islands, and you wonder how someone had swam and scaled its walls which are 20 feet high. But then you see a lot of seagulls with red dipped on their yellow beak tips coming back and forth toward the island like it’s a rotting whale, and you realize that it isn’t trash. It’s the black and white downy feathers of their young. You want to climb onto the island and lie on the grass because you know that no one else has ever been there. On the beaches are dark green and rubbery kelp, hollow yellow-brown bulbs, white driftwood, and the fishy decay of jellyfish and seashells. When the tide ebbs, the water sizzles and bubbles over the coarse sand like spit on the mouth of a crab, and the rounded glass and pebbles crunch underfoot, such as when you dig your hand into a jar of marbles.

She is running ahead of me, dancing just beyond the last unfoldings of waves along the sand.

“No rules,” she says. “No rules!” 

She is wearing a black hoodie with a white zipper, blue jeans, and gray Converse sneakers. She is wearing her black-framed glasses that are big for her face. Her black hair is rich and mane-like and begins at her widow’s peak and reaches the middle of her back. I have spent many nights with my nose in its roots. An old pair of leather binoculars from the inn hang from her neck, as they do on mine. They are heavy and cloudy, and we have brought them for show. I am laughing as I record her, thinking about how we will watch this video in the future after we have spent many years away from each other, after we realize that in spite of having met when we were so young, we hadn’t yet met anyone like the other.

“I don’t think you’re taking advantage of ‘No Rules,’” she says.

Then she lopes sideways towards me to avoid the lens. I lower it and we embrace.

What a photo or a video can do is anchor a memory, like a historical fact. It can keep it from wear or corruption. And when you look at it after many years, after the bramble of memory has obscured it, the truth emerges. We should be suspicious of our memories because I read that every act of remembrance alters the memory itself, like a game of telephone. But a memory feeds on remembrance, so either way it’s doomed.

That video of Alex on the beach is gone now. Before leaving for my trip I erased it from my computer because I wanted to feel something. I did, I felt a stupid kind of loss. And now she’s leaving my head, losing dimension. That makes me kind of sad. But grateful too, that a memory has a half-life, but that the way she made me feel will live as long as I do.

There’s a photo from that weekend, too. I remember the nonchalant way that she gave it to me after she’d had the roll developed, like I could take it or leave it. I think she wanted me to take it. We’re in our room in the old water tower. It was one of the nicer rooms in the small boutique hotel. I think it was called the Raven Room. I remember that I’d tried to get the heater to work, but I just couldn’t figure it out. So it was pretty cold. There’s the fact that you have to live through every minute of every day with the disease of the knowledge inside you. If you were me, you liked to plan and make lists—the dozen journals you’ve kept since you were sixteen because you decided you were going to become a writer are filled mostly with the prestige you hoped to have in ten years. You want to burn, quietly, lastingly, like you were walking a long journey to deliver lost children to their parents. But every minute of every day passes you by like you're running away from someone in a dream. You can't grip the ground, you seem to bounce. Your punches seem to take place underwater. One day you find the solution—it’s your own little pet you keep in your coat pocket. You feed it and pet it and watch it grow. One evening, sunset, you go to the benches by the bike cage for a smoke like you do every night. Then you go back to the room that you have to share with kids who don't get it, a room that you’ve taken out a big loan to pay for because it costs you two grand a month to share it with another grown man who talks to his long-distance girlfriend every night under his sheets. You wonder if that debt is real or if it’s more like a cancer that’ll form within you in your fifties. You remember how you used to sit by the creek with the red-orange crawfish and the gangly tadpoles and how you’d stroke the moss by you, lost in the thought that it might be the canopy of a miniature forest, under which were miniature jaguars and poison dart frogs and pyramids made of gold. When you were little, you always thought there was more to something than there really was. You believed in the whole universe that movies and books created. Man, you’d give anything to go live in that dream—waking up cold at night next to her and the no-better rightness of the sleepy ache as you fit your limbs into hers, then the cabin and the stove and the snow that makes your face sting and your thoughts clean, then the fold between the fat wrists and fists of your children and the pride of being a father—

So you jump, man, right from the sixth floor. You see the ground come at you way too quickly, a bark escapes your throat, you land on your neck and lie there broken, all your joints pointing the wrong way like a goddamn Picasso. What are you thinking about? Hold on, you think, hold on, I'm having some new thoughts, and I need more time ...

You know it only takes you four seconds to fall from the Golden Gate? 245 feet, 75 miles per hour. Four-story buildings are only, what, forty feet? I don't know the math, but I don't think that's a lot of time to think or a lot of height to reach a fast speed.

You’re in the water now—coldest water you’ve ever been in, if your spine still works—but you’re pretty much egg drop soup at this point. Only thing left intact is your brain, which is the one thing you were trying to shut up. You’re deep, deep in the ocean, unable to paddle or see or hear or feel. Just float. Maybe you got a minute. I don't know how many times I’ve been burned trying to sound smart about something and finding out that he’s gone a lot further in that thing than I have. One time I was over at Ben’s house and everyone was playing video games in this game room where they had a couch and a bunch of superfast computers. His folks had a really nice big house in one of those gated communities where every house looks the same. They had a pool, too, and I was thinking he’d had a good childhood. Brian and Ben were back from college so the whole family was there. Brian had this idea of the three brothers sneaking up on Bernard and scaring the shit out of him. Been an accident, come as soon as possible. Man, she knows. Because they’d tell her if he was going to be alright. She's in a dream when they take her downstairs. Bernard died right there, like the intersection of the concrete squares where Henry landed. I think it would be good to put an engraving on the spots where people have died, just a little symbol or an initial and a 1993-2015 or something. Then when you're walking around all caught up in your thoughts, you might see one and it'd make you think for a bit. But I guess if you did that here, there’d be engravings all over. When someone dies in a car or bike accident, they'll paint a bike white and chain it to a pole near where the person got hit or they'll make a mini Catholic shrine. You see them a lot when you're driving bendy mountain roads. There'll be flowers and little trinkets with a photo of the dead person. Usually, it's a Hispanic person, and usually they're young. Young kid like Bernard, why take him? What were all his brains for, all the tennis lessons and the college applications and his great confidence if he was just going to be taken away like that?

She told her sister she should get her pipes looked at because a couple times in the middle of the night she had woken up gasping. Couple days later her sister died in her sleep. I remember my mom told me this while I was walking past her in the living room. I stopped to listen to her and she began to cry. She said it could have been her who died. She asked me if I thought God would have sent her to hell. She said she didn’t think so, that it didn’t matter who she was or what she had or hadn’t done because she was so young. I thought of my mom as a child, youngest of seven, wide-eyed in mortal fear as she stood at the doorway to the bedroom where her sister lay. The whole family huddled in there quiet and crying. I thought of her sister who was eternally young in my mind and who must have looked like my mother when she was young.

My mom’s parents owned an undergarments shop and put their kids to work. Someone they knew told her parents that her older brother had a brain and should go to school. He had wanted to go, too. “When can I go to school?” My mom remembers him asking his dad. But he didn’t let him. Instead he had him deliver undergarments by bicycle every morning. One winter he got a bad cough and died.

I walked back home from Unit 2 along Dwight and past People’s Park. It’s grim walking by that park at night. You think about the myths they gave us about how in the 60s this park was full of all these fresh kids with big glasses and high-waisted pants protesting for free speech or civil rights. Now you’ll hardly see a student there, just homeless people with their shopping carts and blue tarps setting up camp for the night. Black garbage bags split open and ragged clothes all over the sidewalk, those cardboard takeout boxes with rotting food all mashed and thrown around. Sometimes needles. People howling, people laughing and talking to themselves. You’ll hardly see a girl walking by that park at night, and if you do she’ll practically run by it.

Nowadays there aren’t too many trees in that park, just stumps. I was guessing they’d been cut down pretty recently because there were a lot of cockroaches sniffing and feeling around. That’s what happens when you chop down a lot of trees. All the roaches that lived in them were roaming around now. There was one right by my foot. I bent down to get a good look at the guy. Shiny, gummy, amber-brown thing with thorny legs and grooves on its back like a fingerprint. Missing the last half of one of his legs, thing was just hanging off the ground. He was waving his feelers curiously at my foot. Some people really hate the critters, but they don’t give me the same reaction that those big jungle centipedes and the potato bugs with their fat juicy yellow legs do. Cockroaches, they’re like scarab beetles. They look like very fine machines to me. There’s something analytical, thoughtful even, about the way they move their feelers, and the way they’ll respond to your vibrations. Even their heads look like they’ve been clicked into place, and that they’d spark and be a mess of chips and wires if you stepped on one of them. I did my best to avoid that, though, all the way down Dwight till I got back onto Telegraph. Aashik and Axel’s apartment was right where Berkeley turns into Oakland, where Telegraph starts to die.

All the way back I was thinking about my mom’s dead brother going to school for the first time. I bet he’d have a couple new pencils and some new clothes, or maybe just some hand-me-downs that his mom washed and ironed for him the night before. Thought about his eight brothers and sisters buzzing around him as he eats a sturdy breakfast that morning, teasing him. But inside, they’re proud. My mom not really understanding what’s going on but zipping around on the mood of it all. His dad and his older brothers trying to give him advice. Thought about him sitting up straight at his desk before school starts while all the other kids are running around. There’s a freshly sharpened pencil lined up on it, and he loves that thing—how sharp the tip is, how pristine the pink eraser—and he promises himself he’s going to work as hard as he can because it sure beats riding his bike every morning.





CHAPTER 13

WE WENT OUT to San Francisco that night with Aashik’s friends, Polk Street. I always have these expectations for bars, and then you go to them and no one even talks to anyone outside their group, at least in the cities. Then even within your group all people want to talk about is some meal they had, like they’ve just had a religious experience with the damn Pieta. Like one of Aashik’s friends was telling me that he was tired of giving his soul to this company that was about to roll out self-driving cars. He was a sincere Indian guy, small and skinny with a nice smile and brown, curly hair. One of those guys who looked like he could be anywhere from 18 or 28. Anyway, he tells me he wants out. That got me excited, you know, I was going, Yes man, get the hell outta here.

“What are you going to do?” I asked him. “You probably got enough savings to do whatever the hell you want. You gonna have a great adventure?”

“Man, I want to eat good food,” he said. “I want to travel the world and eat good food.”

“That’s it?”

“Yeah. Have you ever been to Singapore?”

“No.”

“You have to go to Singapore and try the satay there. Literally heaven in my mouth.”

This damn fool, I thought. You’d think that food was the greatest good we ever got. You think a Michelin-Star satay would have stopped Henry from jumping? Dangle that shit out the window and tell him to come off the ledge? 

There was a girl with us who was one of Aashik’s friends from one of his business classes. She was interested in me from the beginning of the night, and I figured I could use it. We went from bar to bar, and when I went outside for some air she followed me out and was familiar with me like we’d been going out for a while. She had a big chest and she kept brushing up against me. She was like a little girl with her high voice. She’d just finished an internship at Nielsen, where they do all the TV ratings, and she’d just gotten something full time at Unilever, that company whose fuzzy “U” you see on everything, from shampoo bottles to peanut butter jars. Data analysis, she said. Wow, I said. She told me all of this in that very generous voice that successful people have when they’re talking about their careers. She asked me what I was up to. Not much, I said. But then I told her a little about my travels and the scars on my thighs, and there was a lurch. I want to see them, she said, and brought her chest to me again.

Then after some drinks we went over the bridge back to her apartment. Our driver was an older guy who drove sometimes on the weekends for some extra money. He had a little trouble with how to get onto the Bay Bridge and how to get off, and the girl was very sure with the directions. You’re a superstar, he told her. He knew what was going on and kept hinting that he was really happy for me, and I tried to buy into it too but couldn’t because we were about to go use each other. After some wine in foam cups we went to her bed and when I finished the air was metallic and after I washed I left her apartment and walked out down a hill back home. Felt like I needed to have a real human moment so I hit up a girl named Natalie who I knew was having a birthday party at her pre-med frat. I went over and headed right for the liquor. I was already messed up but you can always use some more when you’re at a new place. There were still a lot of people at the party, but it was that part of the night where drinking more just makes you dead in the eyes. I go sit across from these two girls on a couch. We were talking a little. The older one was an alumna, she was at real med school now, Harvard. Damn, Harvard, I kept saying, and they laughed at that. The younger one was a freshman, cute delicate girl. She wasn’t drinking, and she was being shy. I poured shots and pushed them toward them.

“No,” he says. “She’s had enough.”

I’m guessing he’s talking about the freshman, because the older girl just looks amused. The freshman girl whose name is Sara looks like she wants to disappear.

The guy doesn’t say anything, just stands right in front of me.

“I’m just trying to have a drink with your friends, man. I’m not trying to rape them.”

By now, there are a couple of his friends leering over at us. Man, when I said that, I might as well have said that I was trying to rape them. They got all excited, all primal, like I was the goddamn devil, man, and I was wondering if they could smell it on me. Then one of them, big guy, came up to me and asked me who I knew here.

I just wanted to show them I wasn’t a bad guy, and so I turned to Elliott and asked him if he wanted to go outside to talk it out. I just wanted to make a goddamn real connection, man, just have a real goddamn human moment. But everyone there took it to mean that I wanted to fight the guy, and finally Natalie told me it’d be best if I left. She talked to me outside for a minute, telling me that Elliott and Sara went way back to when they were kids, had grown up together and all that. Well, how the hell was I supposed to know that I was playing an extra in their goddamn Korean drama? But I was so messed up that I just told her okay, okay I get it. I decided to run back home, as fast as I could, falling over a couple times. Couple miles later I entered the apartment. I couldn’t breathe, felt like someone was squeezing my brain. My heart was going real fast, man, thought I was having a heart attack, and I was spiralling bad, trying to bottom out by telling myself things that I was sure about, trying to find some footing. But I was a freak, man, I knew it, and so did everyone else.

Day after day, night after night, year after year.

“I don't know.”

“What happened?”

“I’m a piece of shit, man. I’m delusional. I have nothing, man, nothing.”

CHAPTER 14

THE DAY AFTER that night with Aashik, I was on the John Muir Trail. I figured it was time to head back to LA to my parents, and I thought I’d do a long hike to get there. I took a train to Merced and hopped on a bus to get to Yosemite Valley. 

I had a lot of extra stuff with me because I was moving, like my laptop and clothes and books, so I was packing close to 50 lbs. Man, I really overestimated my strength there. First day was a half day, so it was alright. It was the beginning of a long climb, something like a mile-gain over 15 miles. There were a lot of day hikers on the trail, too. Up, up, up. Second day was where it hurt. I’d banged my knee pretty good when I fell running back to the apartment, so I made a wooden staff and used it like a crutch whenever I went downhill. Man, crawling up, up, up while you’re surrounded by trees. Not so many people around to talk to, either. I thought I was hearing voices in the scratch of squirrel claws gripping tree bark or the gurgling streams, whispers in the brush. Thought I was seeing tents where there were boulders and hikers where there were saplings. Eyes in the dark knots of aspens. I had to stop every ten minutes to rest, too. I didn’t know how to properly adjust my pack so it was hanging off me and killing my shoulders. It was so heavy for me that I had to prop it up and sit down to get the straps on. My head was becoming a bad place. I got to a point where I was so tired that I got my sleeping bag out and went inside it for a few minutes just a couple feet off the trail. But I was thinking I’d be embarrassed if someone saw me like that, so I took a couple bites from a bar and kept going. Five minutes later, Tuolumne Meadows opened up to me.

Between the grandfatherly granite domes on either side, brown brooks cut through a plain of tall lush grass carrying water so clear that you only knew it was there because the sun glittered on it, and so appetizing that when you leaned over to cup it to your lips, you felt stronger. There was a great feeling of vitality in the meadow, as if the sun shone more brightly upon it, as if the rich black soil was denser with nutrients, the water with more minerals. It was in the sweet cold air, the kind that you breathe in after a snowfall, in the green pines that were strong and healthy, and in the glossy, athletic deer that grazed and sprung lightly from each other. It was in all the colors, too. The sky was bluer and deeper, the clouds richer, the wildflowers with their plump, velvet petals concentrating blues, whites, oranges, and purples in a way I only thought the birds and fishes of the tropics could. I walked through that meadow in a daze that something like this was real, and that I was in it. Then I thought about what Aashik had done for me, and tears came down my face. I promised myself that someday I would repay him. As I made my way south through the Sierras over the next two weeks this feeling visited me every day, and I’d have to sit on a fallen tree or a boulder and wait for it to pass.





CHAPTER 15

SUMMER OF NASHVILLE, ‘17, you gave me your copy of Rilke’s Letters to a Young Poet and said that no one writes letters like this anymore. A few weeks before that, in our dorm at Lutsen, I shared with you an idea I had for a series of little books: Through the course of my life I’d try to convince you of the existence of God. You got up from your bed and came over to mine, which made me sit up fast. There was a fire in your eyes as you paced our shabby room, something I hadn’t seen in you for a long time. Well, this is the best I got. I’m no Rilke, and I got no arguments of reason. You’ve heard them all, and I don’t want to debate you on that because you would probably win. Nor do I want to rattle off a list of points for the proof of the resurrection. All I’ve got for you are some stories. 

It’s been five years since that night with Aashik, and I still don’t know exactly what happened, only that I keep coming back to it. Here are two more stories for you. It’s okay if you still think I’m full of shit. I figure I got about 50 years to get it right, and it’s not looking like I’ll be getting a 9 to 5 anytime soon.





CHAPTER 16

COUPLE MONTHS AGO I was at my brother’s wedding. It was a very fine wedding. My brother rented out an entire restaurant at Bryant Park. We took our photos in front of the steps of the New York Public Library, at dinner there was steak, the waitstaff was offering plates of hors d'oeuvres, and my wine glass was fat the whole night. It was a rare experience for someone like me who doesn’t make much money and lives with his parents.

I was one of the groomsmen and the ring-bearer. It had been humid the day before, but today it was cool, and the evening was coming. There were about a hundred guests seated on the terrace overlooking a large lawn of grass on which hundreds of New Yorkers were picnicking. Before them lay a stage above which a banner read: OTHELLO 7PM. I was wearing a tuxedo that my brother had sent to me a week prior to secure its fit. My brother and his bride read their vows, something about loading the dishwasher and closing the kitchen cabinets, and while the other groomsmen beamed and chuckled towards them, I looked away.

The last meaningful encounter I had with him before the wedding happened about a year ago when he came home for a week. I’d just gotten back to California after hitchhiking from Nashville. He came into my room one day just before I was going out for a run and asked me where his hiking pack was. I told him that I had sold it to my friend. That was you. He hadn’t used it in years, and I thought he wouldn’t notice it was gone or that he wouldn’t care because he made a lot of money. He was angry with me. I asked him how much it cost. He told me I owed him $150, so I gave it to him in cash. He kept telling me not to touch his stuff. I told him that since I had just paid him, that was the end. He wouldn’t stop, though, said he was only angry because I had sold it. It’s the principle, he said. Man, I was thinking about how I was working from sunup to midnight putting up Christmas lights for $15 bucks an hour now, and I didn’t want to hear him talk about principle. I felt at that moment the urge to be violent toward him. You greedy fucking bastard, I said and left. “James’ brother,” he said to me. We shook hands. He asked me what I was drinking. I told him I was going for a whiskey.

“Let’s do a tequila shot,” he said.

So we did, with the lime and the salt.
